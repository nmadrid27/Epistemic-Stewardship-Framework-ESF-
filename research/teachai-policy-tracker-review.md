# TeachAI Policy Tracker: Working Review

Source: [TeachAI Policy Tracker](https://www.teachai.org/policy-tracker) + [Toolkit Principles](https://www.teachai.org/toolkit-principles) + [Foundational Policy Ideas](https://www.teachai.org/policy)

## What TeachAI Is

TeachAI is a coalition (ISTE, Code.org, Khan Academy, WEF, others) that tracks 50+ U.S. state and 15+ country AI education policies and provides a toolkit for schools developing AI guidance. Their seven principles and the AI Assessment Scale (Perkins et al.) are the backbone of their recommendations.

## The Seven Principles

1. **Purpose**: Use AI to help all students achieve educational goals
2. **Compliance**: Adhere to existing privacy/data policies (FERPA, COPPA, GDPR)
3. **Knowledge**: Promote AI literacy
4. **Balance**: Realize benefits, address risks
5. **Integrity**: Advance academic integrity
6. **Agency**: Maintain human decision-making
7. **Evaluation**: Regularly assess AI impacts

## Verification Notes

Each gap claim below was cross-checked against the full text of the seven principles page (teachai.org/toolkit-principles) and the foundational policy ideas page (teachai.org/policy). Verification pass conducted 2026-03-02.

- [CONFIRMED] No principle distinguishes between experienced and novice users needing different processes. Principle 4 lists separate risks for students and teachers but applies identical guardrails to both.
- [CONFIRMED] No pre-drafting or pre-engagement step appears anywhere in the seven principles or toolkit.
- [CONFIRMED] The AI Assessment Scale describes permission levels only. At Level 3 ("AI Collaboration"), the instruction says "directing AI to achieve your goals while demonstrating your critical thinking" but provides no methodology for how to do this.
- [CONFIRMED] Principle 6 (Agency) states values ("humans in the loop," "ultimate control with the user") but provides no specific decision-making framework or operational method.
- [CONFIRMED] Teacher intellectual ownership of their own work is not addressed. Teacher AI use is framed through operations and efficiency.
- [CONFIRMED] No principle uses the terms "epistemic," "ownership of thinking," "intellectual ownership," "creative direction," or "forming your own position." The closest language is Principle 5's "work that is genuinely their own," which is an authenticity claim, not a process.
- [PARTIAL] Principle 4 does acknowledge teachers and students face "different challenges," which is closer to the ESF split than the other principles. But "different challenges" stays at the risk level and never becomes "different processes." This is noted in the "What TeachAI Does Well" section.
- [LIMITATION] The policy tracker itself is a directory of links, not a database of policy content. Individual state and country documents were not reviewed. It's possible some individual policies make the production/development distinction. This review covers TeachAI's own framework and toolkit, not every linked policy.

## Gaps the ESF Is Trying to Address

### 1. One framework for everyone

The seven principles apply uniformly. A 20-year veteran teacher and a first-year student get the same guidance. The principles never ask: does this person already have the judgment to evaluate AI output, or are they still building it?

Principle 6 (Agency) says "maintain human decision-making" and keep "humans in the loop." But it treats all humans as interchangeable. The senior designer who knows which of forty comps to ship and the first-year student who can't tell why a composition works are both just "humans" in this framing.

### 2. Permission levels instead of process models

The AI Assessment Scale embedded in Principle 5 (Integrity) defines five levels:

1. No AI (controlled environment)
2. AI Planning (brainstorming, outlining)
3. AI Collaboration (students direct AI)
4. Full AI (drafting, feedback, refinement)
5. AI Exploration (creative problem-solving)

This is a **permission scale**: how much AI to allow. Not a **process model**: how to maintain ownership at any level. The scale answers "how much AI can I use?" but never asks "how do I stay the author of my thinking while using it?"

A student given Level 3 permission ("direct AI to achieve assessment goals") has no methodology for what "directing" means. No pre-drafting step. No checkpoint questions. No way to tell if they're directing or following.

### 3. The problem is framed as cheating, not as development

Principle 5 (Integrity) frames the AI challenge through academic dishonesty: plagiarism, cheating, attribution. The recommended responses are about restructuring assignments and teaching citation formats (MLA, APA, Chicago for AI sources).

This misses the deeper problem. A student who properly cites AI and still can't explain why they made the choices in their work hasn't cheated. They've just failed to develop. The integrity frame catches fraud but misses drift.

### 4. No pre-drafting mechanism

Nothing in the seven principles or the toolkit addresses what happens **before** AI is engaged. No concept of establishing your position first. No directive memo. No requirement that the human have a documented starting point to compare AI output against.

The closest is Principle 6 (Agency): "AI serves as a supportive guide while still leaving ultimate control with the user." But "ultimate control" without a documented starting position is just retroactive endorsement. You approved it, but you can't demonstrate you directed it.

### 5. Teacher use is framed as operations, not intellectual work

When the toolkit discusses teacher AI use, the examples are: lesson planning, content differentiation, assessment design, grading, scheduling. These are framed as efficiency gains.

Missing: the teacher as intellectual author of their curriculum, course design, and pedagogical choices. A teacher using AI to develop a syllabus faces the same ownership question as anyone else producing high-stakes intellectual work with AI. The toolkit doesn't address this.

### 6. "Responsible use" without a methodology

The toolkit provides guardrails (what to allow, what to restrict) and values (honesty, trust, fairness). It does not provide a workflow. There is no structured process for maintaining ownership of thinking while using AI. It tells you to be responsible but doesn't give you a method for doing it.

## What TeachAI Does Well

- **Comprehensive coverage**: 50+ states, 15+ countries. The most complete policy directory available.
- **Principle 4 (Balance)** separately lists student risks and teacher risks, acknowledging they face different challenges. But "different risks" doesn't become "different processes."
- **Honest about limitations**: Recommends against AI detection tools (false positives), which is a good call.
- **Evaluation mindset**: Principle 7 emphasizes continuous assessment and policy revision. Acknowledges this is evolving.

## Usable in Social Post 02

The TeachAI framework is a clear, well-intentioned example of the exact mistake Post 02 describes: one policy for everyone. The AI Assessment Scale is the most specific illustration. Five levels of permission, zero process for maintaining ownership at any of them.

Not adversarial. TeachAI is doing good work on coverage, literacy, and compliance. The gap is structural: policy tells you what's allowed but not how to stay the author of your thinking while doing it.
