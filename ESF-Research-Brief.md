# Epistemic Stewardship Framework: A Research Brief

---

## The Problem

You use AI in your work. You approve a draft, and it reads well. Then you realize you cannot point to which ideas are yours and which you accepted because they sounded right.

This is not a failure of discipline. It is a structural problem. Tankelevitch et al. (2024) call it the "illusion of fluency": polished AI output triggers a sense of comprehension even when the reader did not produce the reasoning behind it. The better AI writes, the harder it becomes to distinguish your position from the AI's framing.

Higher education has responded to this with three approaches, each addressing a real concern, none addressing the core problem.

**The ban-or-permit binary.** Most institutions now regulate whether AI can be used and under what conditions. These policies frame AI as an integrity threat. They answer "is this allowed?" but not "how do I maintain ownership of the thinking?"

**The student-only focus.** Frameworks for responsible AI use target students almost exclusively. Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019), in their systematic review of 146 studies, found that faculty as users of AI in their own professional work were largely absent from the research. That gap persists. Faculty appear as implementers of student-facing policies, not as practitioners who need a method for their own work.

**Compliance framing.** Disclosure frameworks structure how AI use should be reported. This matters. But transparency after the fact does not address what happened during the work. A faculty member can disclose every AI contribution while having exercised no intellectual direction over the result.

The result: faculty and students use AI extensively, and no method exists for maintaining intellectual ownership of the work AI helps produce. Not whether AI was used, but whether the human's thinking drove the result.

---

## What ESF Is

The Epistemic Stewardship Framework is a structured method for maintaining genuine intellectual ownership in AI-assisted academic work. It provides specific processes, checkpoints, and documentation so that people who produce work with AI retain ownership of the thinking behind it.

ESF operates at two levels because content production and learning are different activities.

**Level 1** is for faculty, administrators, and institutional leaders who use AI to produce professional work: syllabi, reports, scholarship, assessments, accreditation documents. The workflow is five phases: Scope, Direct, Build, Validate, Disclose. The central mechanism is the Directive Memo: a short document you write before opening any AI tool, capturing your position, emphasis, non-negotiables, and rationale. Once AI gives you a polished draft, your own idea becomes nearly impossible to separate from what the AI proposed. The memo preserves your thinking in its uncontaminated form.

**Level 2** is for students developing the capacity to work with AI while building genuine understanding. The workflow is also five phases, but different ones: Inquire, Position, Explore, Make, Reflect. AI does not enter until Phase 3. Students must articulate what they know and where they stand before AI output exists to react to. This prevents the pattern where students respond to AI suggestions instead of forming their own ideas first.

The levels connect. When a faculty member uses Level 1 to design an assignment, they are simultaneously designing the environment students work within at Level 2. Faculty who require epistemic discipline from students practice it themselves.

Seven constructs support the architecture: the Directive Memo, the Five Questions (a recurring accountability checkpoint), Content Epistemic Weight (a three-tier system for calibrating process to stakes), Human Validation Gates, a Phased Workflow Model, a Disclosure Protocol, and a Framework Evolution Protocol. The framework is tool-agnostic, discipline-agnostic, and designed for adoption within a single academic term.

## How It Works

The Five Questions are the framework's recurring checkpoint. At every transition between phases, the user asks:

| Question | What It Tests |
|----------|--------------|
| Can I defend this? | Could I explain every choice without referencing what the AI said? |
| Is this mine? | Did I direct this, or did I accept the AI's framing because it sounded reasonable? (design authority test) |
| Did I verify? | Have I independently checked facts, sources, and claims? |
| Would I teach this? | Could I stand behind this in front of colleagues or students? |
| Is the disclosure honest? | Does it accurately describe who did what? |

A "no" to any question halts the workflow until the issue is resolved. This is not a reflective exercise. It is a decision gate with a stop condition.

Question 2 catches most people. After an hour of working with AI on a draft, the line between "my position" and "a position I accepted because the AI said it well" gets blurry fast. That is not a weakness. It is how polished output works on your thinking (Tankelevitch et al., 2024).

**Content Epistemic Weight** calibrates the process to the stakes. Not everything needs the full workflow.

| Weight | Examples | What's Required |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| **High** | Scholarship, tenure materials, accreditation reports, exam questions | Full workflow: Directive Memo, all gates, Integrity Report |
| **Medium** | Syllabi, rubrics, assignment briefs, course proposals | Abbreviated memo, key gates, disclosure |
| **Low** | Emails, meeting agendas, formatting tasks | Light review, attribution |

The classification is based on how much human thinking the content demands, not how much AI a policy permits. A tenure portfolio is high-weight because the intellectual claims must originate with the author. A meeting agenda is low-weight because it makes no intellectual claims. This distinction holds regardless of which AI tools exist or what institutional policy says.

For students at Level 2, the process includes additional artifacts: a Position Statement (the student equivalent of the Directive Memo), AI interaction logs, and Records of Resistance, a simple log of what the student kept, revised, and rejected from AI output, with a brief note on why. The rejection column is where students report learning the most about what they actually think. Agreeing with AI teaches nothing. Disagreeing forces you to articulate your position.

## Research Foundation

ESF is grounded in 35 verified scholarly sources across eight research areas. Four findings shaped the framework's design most directly.

**1. Epistemic agency requires structural support.** Clark and Chalmers (1998) established that cognitive tools extend thinking legitimately only when the human actively endorses their contributions. "Active endorsement" is the key condition. Without a documented pre-drafting position, endorsement reduces to "I didn't object," a passive stance their framework does not support. The Directive Memo makes this condition concrete: it captures what you think before the AI offers its version, giving you a standard to endorse against.

Lindebaum et al. (2025) elevate this concern to the institutional level. Drawing on Kant, they argue that widespread AI adoption without structural safeguards produces "organized immaturity": people become passive consumers of AI-generated knowledge rather than active producers and evaluators of knowledge themselves. The danger is not individual laziness. It is institutional design that makes passivity the rational default.

**2. Metacognitive monitoring fails under fluency.** Tankelevitch et al. (2024) provide the most direct empirical evidence for why passive review is insufficient. Their research shows that catching subtle misrepresentation in AI output takes real effort, and that failures of monitoring (accepting output that drifts from your position) are common even among experienced professionals. The Five Questions are designed to trigger the specific metacognitive processes that passive review does not: distinguishing your position from the AI's framing, testing whether you engaged with the content or merely read it.

**3. Faculty are absent from the method.** Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019) documented a striking asymmetry: the vast majority of AI-in-education research addresses student-facing applications. Faculty as practitioners who use AI in their own work receive almost no systematic attention. This gap persists through 2026. Frameworks tell faculty how to manage student AI use. None tells faculty how to manage their own.

**4. Human-first sequencing develops agency.** Three independent research traditions converge on the same structural principle: students must establish their own understanding before AI enters the process. Hutson (2025) requires demonstrated independent proficiency before AI access. Degen and Asanov (2025) found experimentally that students using a Socratic AI tutor (which questions reasoning) showed stronger epistemic agency than those using an AI that answers directly. ESF's Level 2 workflow implements this principle: Phases 1 and 2 are human-only by design.

## Where It Came From

ESF was not derived from a literature review. It was built from practice.

I developed the framework's core constructs through daily use, integrating AI into curriculum development, assessment design, and institutional work at an art and design university. The Directive Memo exists because I found that without one, AI-assisted drafts drifted from my intent in ways that were difficult to detect after the fact. The Five Questions exist because general "review your work" advice was insufficient; specific, answerable questions at specific points in the workflow caught issues that open-ended reflection missed. The two-level architecture exists because a single process model failed when I applied it to both my professional work and my students' learning.

I conducted the literature review after the constructs existed. It serves two purposes: confirming that the problems ESF addresses are real and documented in the field, and identifying where the constructs fill gaps that existing frameworks leave open. Every architectural decision traces back to a problem encountered and solved in practice. The scholarship validates these solutions are not idiosyncratic. But the solutions came first.

This distinction matters for adoption. ESF's constructs are practitioner-tested before they are theoretically grounded. Faculty evaluating the framework are evaluating something that has been used in operational conditions, not something designed from theory and awaiting its first test.

## Getting Started

**Individual.** Write one Directive Memo before your next AI-assisted task. Apply the Five Questions after one draft. See what changes. Scale from there.

**Department.** Select 5 to 10 faculty across disciplines for a one-term pilot. Share results in structured learning sessions. The framework is designed for adoption within a single academic term.

**Institution.** Integrate ESF's constructs into AI policy, student orientation, assessment rubrics, and accreditation documentation. The two-level architecture provides separate entry points for faculty professional development and student learning outcomes.

The full framework, faculty and student toolkits, and implementation guides are open source under a Creative Commons license.
